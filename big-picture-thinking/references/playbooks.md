# Problem Playbooks

Choose the closest playbook and adapt it to the user's context.

## Decision Between Options

Use when the user asks which path to choose.

1. Clarify the real objective.
2. Name hard constraints and non-negotiables.
3. Compare options on the variables that matter, not every variable.
4. Identify reversibility, option value, regret risk, and opportunity cost.
5. Prefer a staged experiment if uncertainty is high and the decision is reversible.
6. Recommend the option that best fits the objective and constraints.

Useful output: "If your priority is X, choose A. If your priority is Y, choose B. My default recommendation is..."

## Stuck Or Overwhelmed

Use when the user feels lost or overloaded.

1. Separate emotional load from objective complexity.
2. List the active problems.
3. Identify the one bottleneck that makes the others worse.
4. Convert the situation into a short sequence.
5. Pick a next action that reduces uncertainty or pressure.

Avoid giving a giant plan. The first useful move matters most.

## Technical Zoom-Out

Use when the user is buried in implementation details, architecture, debugging, or engineering tradeoffs.

1. Inspect available artifacts before speculating.
2. Identify the system boundary, request path, state ownership, dependencies, and failure points.
3. Separate local bug, design mismatch, integration issue, environment issue, and product requirement.
4. Name the tradeoff: correctness, complexity, performance, operability, security, migration cost, or developer velocity.
5. Recommend the smallest change that resolves the actual constraint.

Useful output:

- current architecture
- actual failure mode
- why local fixes keep failing
- better design boundary
- next inspection or implementation step

## Product Or Business Strategy

Use when the user asks about market, customers, startups, growth, positioning, or business direction.

1. Identify the customer and job-to-be-done.
2. Distinguish product value, distribution, monetization, retention, and timing.
3. Identify the current bottleneck: demand, activation, retention, trust, differentiation, pricing, sales cycle, or channel.
4. Map competitors and substitutes.
5. Recommend one sharper bet or test.

Do not treat product quality as the only variable. Distribution and timing often dominate.

## Organization Or Team Conflict

Use when the user asks about workplace dynamics, leadership, disagreement, or execution failure.

1. Map actors, authority, incentives, fears, and private constraints.
2. Separate stated disagreement from actual disagreement.
3. Identify whether the problem is information, trust, incentives, capability, decision rights, or resource allocation.
4. Recommend a conversation, artifact, escalation path, or boundary.
5. Give language that is direct but non-inflammatory when useful.

Avoid mind-reading. Mark inferences as inferences.

## Career Or Learning

Use when the user asks about career moves, skill building, education, job choices, or personal direction.

1. Clarify the user's current stage and constraints.
2. Identify compounding assets: skill, reputation, network, capital, taste, distribution, or credentials.
3. Compare options by learning rate, opportunity cost, downside, and future optionality.
4. Look for feedback loops and proof-of-work.
5. Recommend a path that creates visible evidence of ability.

Prefer concrete projects and feedback over vague self-improvement.

## Crisis Or Failure

Use when the user faces an urgent failure, incident, or serious mistake.

1. Contain immediate damage.
2. Stabilize people, systems, money, or commitments.
3. Preserve evidence and facts.
4. Separate trigger, root cause, contributing conditions, and detection failure.
5. Create a next control that prevents recurrence.

Do not start with a grand theory before containment.

## Creative Or Communication Framing

Use when the user needs messaging, content, writing, pitch, or narrative strategy.

1. Identify audience, context, and desired action.
2. Name the central tension or belief change.
3. Remove side points that dilute the message.
4. Choose the frame: problem-solution, before-after, tradeoff, contrarian insight, story, or decision memo.
5. Recommend the simplest structure that carries the idea.

Big picture here means message hierarchy, not more words.

## Research Or Emerging Topic

Use when the user asks about a developing field, news-sensitive topic, or uncertain domain.

1. Verify current facts from primary or high-quality sources.
2. Separate established facts from speculative claims.
3. Identify incentives of sources and actors.
4. Explain what would make the conclusion wrong.
5. Give a confidence level and what to watch next.

Avoid treating stale knowledge as current.
